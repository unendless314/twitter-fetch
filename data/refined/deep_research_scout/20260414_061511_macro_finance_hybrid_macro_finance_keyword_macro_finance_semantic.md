This issue will become more critical as the private credit market continues to grow.

Title: Central bank inflation models are failing against supply-driven price shocks.
URL: https://x.com/BrianSozzi/status/2043616286278648068
Hidden Assumption: Inflation is primarily a demand-side phenomenon that can be effectively managed by central banks using monetary policy tools like interest rates.
Systemic Gap: The current toolkit of central banks is designed to control demand, but recent inflation is increasingly driven by supply-side shocks (geopolitical conflict, supply chain disruptions, commodity shortages, climate events). Using demand-side tools (rate hikes) to fight supply-side inflation is a critical mismatch, risking policy error by inducing a recession without solving the underlying price pressures.
Required Primitive: A "dual-track macroeconomic policy framework" that distinguishes between demand-pull and cost-push inflation and deploys different tools for each. This would require a new consensus where fiscal policy, industrial policy, and strategic reserves are formally recognized as primary tools for combating supply-shocks, with monetary policy playing a secondary, more targeted role.
Recommended Research Leads: Analyze the stagflation of the 1970s and the policy debates of that era. Study the economic impact of the COVID-19 supply chain disruptions. Research papers on the "Laffer Curve" and the limits of taxation/monetary policy.

Reason: The post itself is just a report of a forecast, but it serves as a perfect example of the systemic issue. The forecast is being raised due to "retail gasoline prices" and "oil prices"—clear supply-side factors. Yet the institutional and market response still centers almost exclusively on the Federal Reserve's reaction. This reveals a dangerous cognitive inertia in economic policy, which is likely to be severely tested over the next five years as supply volatility becomes the norm, not the exception.
