Title: Housing as an Investment Vehicle vs. a Social Good Creates Systemic Instability
URL: https://x.com/DavidPocock/status/2028220961833754858
Hidden Assumption: Policies that encourage property investment (e.g., tax concessions) are beneficial for the economy and do not systemically undermine housing affordability for others.
Systemic Gap: The post highlights a fundamental policy contradiction: governments want housing to be both an affordable necessity and a high-returning investment. RBA data shows these goals are mutually exclusive, as investor lending directly fuels price increases and locks out first-time buyers, creating a feedback loop that increases interest rate sensitivity and systemic risk.
Required Primitive: A formal policy framework that decouples the "shelter" value of housing from its "speculative" value. This could involve new tax structures (e.g., land value taxes), dual-track housing markets (one for residents, one for investors), or different capital requirements for investor vs. residential mortgages.
Recommended Research Leads: Study the long-term effects of Singapore's HDB (Housing & Development Board) model. Model the macroeconomic impact of phasing out property tax concessions for investors.
Stance: support
Reason: This challenges the politically entrenched narrative that "what's good for property investors is good for the economy." It identifies a core driver of inequality and financial fragility that is directly tied to government policy and central bank data, making it a critical, long-term research area.
