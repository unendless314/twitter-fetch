Title: Structural Changes in Investment Have Weakened Monetary Policy's Power
URL: https://x.com/int_mon_econ/status/2040336288268603678
Hidden Assumption: Central banks can reliably control consumption and labor income by adjusting interest rates, because investment's response to rates will create a predictable, amplified effect on the real economy.
Systemic Gap: The entire transmission mechanism of monetary policy, a cornerstone of post-war economic management, is weakening due to fundamental, secular changes in the global economy (lower labor share in investment goods, higher import share, shift to less interest-rate-sensitive investment like software). Central banks are using a less effective primary tool.
Required Primitive: A new framework for monetary policy that either (1) finds new transmission channels to influence the real economy or (2) explicitly models the "leakage" from structural changes, leading to different policy calibrations (e.g., requiring larger rate moves for the same effect, or using fiscal/industrial policy as a primary tool).
Recommended Research Leads: Analyze the elasticity of modern investment categories (cloud infrastructure, IP, R&D) to interest rates vs. traditional capex. Model the economic impact of re-shoring investment goods production on monetary policy effectiveness. Quantify the "import leakage" of monetary policy by country.

This isn't just a "risk-off" or "risk-on" environment; it's a "correlation breakdown" environment, where traditional diversification fails and quantitative models give nonsensical outputs.
Required Primitive: A "correlation state" or "market regime" model that can account for periods where all assets become positively correlated due to an overwhelming macro factor (e.g., a massive liquidity injection, a declaration of war, a sovereign debt crisis). This goes beyond simple volatility targeting and requires a qualitative overlay on quantitative models.
Recommended Research Leads: Study historical periods of "correlation breakdown" (e.g., October 1987, March 2020). Develop a factor model where the dominant factor can be "geopolitical panic" or "sovereign default fear," causing all assets to be priced based on their liquidity and proximity to the crisis epicenter rather than their intrinsic cash flows.

Reason: This anomaly points to a deep flaw in the plumbing of modern finance. Most automated trading, risk management, and portfolio construction systems are not designed for this scenario. It reveals that during extreme duress, all assets may simply be a claim on a failing system, and they all start moving together. Understanding this dynamic is crucial for surviving the next major financial crisis, making it highly relevant in 2031.
